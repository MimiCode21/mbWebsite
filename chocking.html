How to Avoid Choking

Try these simple techniques to help you on your food journey

Ensuring the safety of infants is a top priority for parents and caregivers.
Choking hazards are a significant concern during an infant's early stages of development,
especially when they begin to explore solid foods.


Here are the top 10 foods to avoid when introducing solids to infants to reduce the risk of choking.

Whole Grapes

Grapes are one of the most common choking hazards for infants.
Their smooth, slippery skin and round shape make them difficult to grasp and potentially block the airway.

How to prepare:

Hotdogs

Hotdogs are another high-risk food due to their shape and texture.
They can easily get lodged in an infant's throat. If serving hotdogs, be sure to cut them lengthwise and then into small, bite-sized pieces.

How to prepare:

Nuts and Seeds

Whole nuts and seeds pose a choking risk because they are hard and small.
Avoid serving these to infants. Instead, opt for nut butters (such as peanut butter) spread thinly on crackers or bread.

How to prepare:

Raw Carrots

Raw carrots are firm and can break into small, hard pieces when chewed.
Cooked and finely grated or mashed carrots are safer options.

How to prepare:

Popcorn

Popcorn is a high-risk food because it can easily become lodged in an infant's throat.
Avoid giving popcorn to children under the age of four.

How to prepare:

Hard Candy

Hard candies are obvious choking hazards due to their size and hardness. Keep them out of reach of infants and toddlers.

How to prepare:

Chunks of Meat or Cheese

Large pieces of meat or cheese can be challenging for infants to chew and swallow safely.
Ensure these foods are cut into small, manageable pieces.

How to prepare:

Chewing Gum

Chewing gum is not suitable for infants or young children as it can be easily swallowed whole.

How to prepare:

Marshmallows

The sticky texture of marshmallows can make them difficult to swallow.
Avoid giving them to infants until they are older and can chew more effectively.

How to prepare:

Stringy Vegetables

Vegetables with long, stringy fibers, like celery,
should be avoided until infants have the necessary jaw and tongue coordination to manage them safely.

How to prepare:



In addition to avoiding these foods, always supervise infants closely while they eat, and encourage them to sit upright during meals. Cut food into small, manageable pieces, and pay attention to the development and readiness of your child for solid foods. When introducing new foods, consider their size, texture, and your child's ability to handle them safely. This precautionary approach helps reduce the risk of choking and ensures a safe and enjoyable transition to solid foods for your infant.
